### 22. Anything else we should include? (proud of / concerned about) ⚠️

```
What I am proudest of is the transformation of our introductory programming course at scale. Since I took over CS1 (now CS 124) in Fall 2017, enrollment has roughly doubled to over 2,000 students per year (about 80% non-majors); A-range grades rose from about 50% to 80% while failures fell from about 5% to 2.5%; drop rates fell and downstream performance rose; and performance gaps between genders, between majors and non-majors, and across prior-experience levels closed or disappeared — all while rigor increased, moving from a single proctored paper final to weekly autograded programming under proctored, computer-based conditions. Over 21 terms at Illinois I have taught 16,107 students.

These gains rest on software I build and release openly. learncs.online provides a free, public version of the course: 61 daily lessons in Java and Kotlin, 902 runnable playgrounds, 2,540 interactive walkthroughs contributed by 264 instructors, and over 700 autograded problems, used well beyond Illinois (e.g., Northeastern, community colleges, and high-school outreach). Supporting systems include Jeed (secure untrusted-code execution ~1000x faster than alternatives), Questioner (autograder authoring an order of magnitude faster), and ops-class.org. I have also recruited and mentored more than 2,000 course staff and helped found the Illinois Computer Science Summer Teaching Workshop, now in its fifth year.

I am now rebuilding computing education for a world where AI coding agents write most of the code. I redesigned CS 124 around AI coding agents (each student designs and builds their own Android app with an agent, with classical programming still assessed under proctored conditions); created CS 199 UAI: Using and Understanding AI for non-technical students (continuing as CS 108) with a novel two-agent conversational-assessment system; am designing CS 199 ASK: Conversational Programming; and am leading the design of a proposed four-year Applied Computing degree spanning the Siebel School, Art & Design, Gies, and the iSchool. This work has drawn recognition beyond Illinois through the Replit Faculty Fellowship and selection as a Faculty AI Fellow.

Looking ahead, the most pressing question facing our field is what computing education should become now that capable AI is everywhere: what to teach when AI can write most of the code, how to teach students to direct AI agents well, and how to keep assessment honest and authentic. I believe the School is unusually well positioned to lead here, and that secure, AI-resistant computer-based assessment and AI-fluent curriculum should be institutional priorities.
```
