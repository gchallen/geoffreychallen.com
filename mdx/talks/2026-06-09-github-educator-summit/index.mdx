## Abstract

A 20-minute lightning talk opening the GitHub Education Educator Summit's Day 0 *Changing Curriculum* workshop. Two worked examples from Spring 2026: an assignment-level redesign of CS 124 around independent, AI-collaborative student projects with time-on-AI grading; and Using and Understanding AI, a new course for non-technical students built entirely through conversational programming, anchored by multi-agent conversational assessment. Both are arguments for the same shape — let AI in deliberately, redesign the work around what only the student can do, and grade engagement instead of artifact.

## Framing

This talk is meant to seed an afternoon workshop, not stand alone. The audience is a room of CS educators — mostly making course-level and assignment-level decisions, not program-level ones — about to spend several hours in a hands-on curriculum-redesign workshop. The goal of the lightning talk is to give them concrete, replicable evidence at two scales that can become starting material for the work that follows.

**Goals.** Show what redesign actually looks like in practice. Argue for one consistent shape across both worked examples: let AI in deliberately, redesign the work around what only the student can do, grade engagement instead of artifact. Hand off to the workshop with the open question that the rest of the summit picks up — *what is the right balance between classical fundamentals and agentic development, and what specifically makes someone a good AI collaborator?*

## Assignments: CS 124 in Spring 2026

The shape of a programming assignment has been broken for a year. The multi-week Android assignment we had been giving introductory CS students at Illinois was, in July 2025, completable by Claude Code in an hour with only the test suite in hand. A Fall 2025 partial fix — blur the spec, give students less to hand to AI — failed; the spec is the part students need to do the work fairly, and that same information is enough for AI.

In Spring 2026, ~400 CS 124 students each designed and built their own Android app in collaboration with Claude Code. The first half of the project is ideation, peer feedback, and brainstorming feasibility in conversation with Claude. The second half is structured building sessions. Seven weeks of design work precede the first line of implementation code. Because no two projects are the same, we grade for time on AI — instrumented from session logs, with AI itself distinguishing substantive engagement from off-task chatter. Classical programming fundamentals stay rigorous in parallel — 15 hours of proctored testing per semester in the Illinois CBTF, mastery learning, frequent small assessment with rolling retakes — alongside the project-based work.

The honest punchline: we still don't know what the right balance between classical fundamentals and agentic development should be. That is the open question — and it's the question Day 2 picks up.

> **Draft note — beats.** Spec broken (July 2025 Claude demo) → Fall 2025 blurred-spec failure (with student quotes) → Spring 2026 redesign (idea pipeline, weekly agentic activities, time-on-AI grading) → classical fundamentals coexist in the CBTF → the open question.

## Courses: Using and Understanding AI

Launched this semester for non-technical students. No prerequisites, no code, AI literacy as general education. The course infrastructure was built entirely through conversational programming — not a single line manually read, written, or debugged — across 199 sessions, 4,038 turns, and 29,770 tool calls. The build is documented at <https://usingandunderstanding.ai/create>.

The pedagogical move is to let AI in everywhere it removes a barrier: inductive sessions with interactive artifacts students play with before we name the mechanism; AI-supported preparation so human-to-human conversation in the room starts on common ground; conversational assessment as the chat-based equivalent of an oral exam — multi-agent, scored against a rubric, validated against adversarial personas (the hardest to defeat was the confident bullshitter). The same recipe — an evaluator agent scored against a rubric — collapses any slow human feedback loop into many fast ones, anywhere it appears.

Several students kept building beyond the course: one replaced apps her sorority pays for; another, an aquascaper, built a tracker for tanks, livestock, plants, water chemistry, and maintenance — a domain I had never heard of before this semester.

> **Draft note — beats.** Course goals (non-technical audience, AI literacy as gen-ed) → built entirely through conversational programming → pedagogical design (inductive artifacts, AI-supported prep, AI-built labs) → conversational assessment (multi-agent, adversarial validation) → the pattern generalizes to any slow feedback loop → final projects that kept going. If pressed for time, drop one of the inductive-exploration deep dives; keep the conversational-assessment story.
